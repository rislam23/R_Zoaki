Cat Information

Basic information on your purrrrfect pet!


Wellness and Vaccines

It is very important to start at a young age or as soon as possible with a rescue cat to see a veterinarian. Cats tend to hide their symptoms and illnesses very well. So early prevention with vaccines is crucial. Kittens can start getting vaccinated at six weeks of age, beginning with boosters for core vaccines of FHCPV (this acronym will vary by veterinary practice and may include herpesvirus, calicivirus, and panleukopenia) and feline leukemia. Rabies vaccines can be administered between four and six months of age and older. Feline immunodeficiency, feline chlamydia and bordetella vaccines are optional depending on need.

Neuter and Spay

Neuter and spay surgery is the removal of the animal's ability to reproduce, thus avoiding possible cancer, infections, unintended pregnancy, and behavioral issues. Most vets would suggest having your cat get this surgery when they are at least six-months-old.

Flea, Tick, and Heartworm and Internal Parasite Prevention

There are many over-the-counter medications, collars, and shampoos that can relieve your cat from those fleas and ticks, as well as prescriptions obtained from a veterinarian. There are also many parasites that could be living inside of your cat. Parasites like hookworms, roundworms, and heartworms can infect a cat from the environment, but are not typical due to a cat's anatomy. Skin parasites like ringworm and mites can also cause problems if you have an outdoor cat. Talk to your veterinarian to determine the right medications to kill and prevent any infestations.

Lifespan & Sleeptime

The lifespan of a domestic cat can range from 10 to 20 years, with an average of 15 years. This will vary based on breed, size, personality, lifestyle, and medical history. Cats are known for their long sleep requirements. Again, the amount of time your cat sleeps will vary based on a number of factors, but expect an average of 16 hours of sleep per day from your feline companion.

Exercise

Although cats are generally thought as lazy, every cat needs some amount of exercise to stay healthy. As you get to know your cat, you will come to understand how much exercise your very individualistic feline needs. The exercise will also tend to follow a routine that fits your cat, often just before mealtimes in the morning and evening.
